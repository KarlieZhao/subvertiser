There Is No Manifesto

Subvertiser.

A revolt against the MBTA’s massive advertising program running across its facilities.

The MBTA (also known as the T) makes about $30 million each year from advertising, which, as stated on its official website, “plays a major role in keeping rides affordable.” At the moment, the T’s advertising program includes

  • ad postings inside and outside of buses, trains, and trolleys;
  • posters and digital screens inside MBTA stations;
  • billboards, display panels and other assets.

That said, walking on or beneath the streets of Boston, the most common things that pop up to commuters are the MBTA’s advertisements!

The T occupies both the physical and mental parts of the public space, simply because it provides citizens public services. However, although the T owns the properties, the train stations, billboards and displays, it does not own the public space. It is not a must for the public to compromise and receive information that celebrates consumption, capitalism, sexism, and questionable values with deceptive visual pollution.

Similar ideas could be found in the work Addendum created by the non-profit arts organization Kadist. Addendum is a browser extension that replaces the ads on your web pages with artworks or, as they call them, “visual essays by artists.” The developers of Addendum talked about their attitude towards online ads: “you downloaded the page, and you own it. It’s yours and you can do whatever you want to it. Just like if you get a free newspaper, you can read it, or cut it up, or burn it. It’s your life and you have no legal obligation to look at every ad presented to you.”

In the city of Boston, we are not the rule-maker. We appreciate the MBTA maximizes its revenues from alternative sources to avoid fare increases, but we do NOT accept that the public areas of Boston are crammed with advertisements. Our campaign can only be done in a tactical way.

By adopting Web AR (augmented reality) technology, we provide commuters an alternative experience to the highly capitalistic, consumeristic information from advertisers. Anyone with a smartphone can scan the QR code in the corners of the ads, and enjoy a digital artwork, a story, or some history that cover the original advertisement. There are more possibilities than ads!
